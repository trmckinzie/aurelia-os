### üí° Definition
> Endogenous chemicals that enable neurotransmission. They transmit signals across a **chemical synapse** from one neuron (pre-synaptic) to another "target" neuron, muscle cell, or gland cell (post-synaptic).

- **The Classification:** They are generally categorized by their effect on the receiving neuron:
    
    - **Excitatory:** Increases the likelihood of the neuron firing (depolarization).
        
    - **Inhibitory:** Decreases the likelihood of the neuron firing (hyperpolarization).
        
    - **Modulatory:** Adjusts the overall sensitivity of large groups of neurons (neuromodulators).

### üìù Key Insight
* - **The "Lock and Key" Principle:** The chemical itself is just a key; the _effect_ depends entirely on the **Receptor** (the lock).
    
    - For example, **Acetylcholine** slows the heart rate down (via muscarinic receptors) but makes skeletal muscles contract violently (via nicotinic receptors).
        
    - This explains why one drug can have wild side effects‚Äîit is hitting the same "key" in different parts of the body where it unlocks different doors.
        
- **The Balance of Power:** Brain health is largely about the ratio of **Glutamate** (Gas) to **GABA** (Brakes).
    
    - Too much Gas = Seizures/Panic.
        
    - Too much Brake = Coma/Sedation.
        
    - Most general anesthetics work by simply flooding the brain with GABA (slamming the brakes) until consciousness turns off.
